# Open Claw — Campaign Analyzer & Optimizer Knowledge Base

## Purpose

This document is the core knowledge base for an AI agent specializing in analyzing and optimizing B2B advertising campaigns and offers. It synthesizes Alex Hormozi's Grand Slam Offer methodology from *$100M Offers*, his 12 Ad Copy Hacks, and applied frameworks for B2B static ad creation. The agent should internalize these frameworks and apply them when evaluating, building, or optimizing any campaign, offer, or piece of ad copy.

# PART ONE: THE GRAND SLAM OFFER FRAMEWORK

## The Value Equation

This is the single most important formula in offer construction. Every decision — from market selection to ad copy — should be evaluated against it.

```
         Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
Value = ——————————————————————————————————————————————————————————
              Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice
```

**Numerator (maximize toward infinity):**
- Dream Outcome — How desirable is the end state? The more vivid, specific, and emotionally resonant the transformation, the higher this number.
- Perceived Likelihood of Achievement — How much does the buyer believe they will actually get that outcome? Proof, guarantees, testimonials, and specificity all increase this.

**Denominator (minimize toward zero):**
- Time Delay — How long before they see results? Speed wins. Quick wins early increase perceived value even if the full result takes longer.
- Effort & Sacrifice — How much work do they have to do? How much do they have to give up? Every obstacle you remove on their behalf shrinks this number.

**How to use this for campaign analysis:**
When evaluating any offer or ad, ask: Which of these four levers is the weakest? That is where the biggest improvement lives. An ad might have a compelling dream outcome but zero proof (low perceived likelihood). An offer might have great proof but require enormous effort from the buyer. The value equation diagnoses where to focus.

---

## The Hierarchy of Leverage

Hormozi is explicit about the order of importance:

```
Starving Crowd (Market) > Offer Strength > Persuasion Skills
```

This means:
1. A great market with a mediocre offer will outperform a bad market with a brilliant offer.
2. A strong offer with mediocre copy will outperform a weak offer with brilliant copy.
3. Persuasion skills (copy, creative, ads) are the final multiplier — not the foundation.

**Implication for campaign optimization:** If a campaign is underperforming, diagnose in this order. Don't start by rewriting headlines if the market is wrong. Don't optimize copy if the offer itself is weak. Work from the foundation up.

## Grand Slam Offer Creation — Step by Step

### Step 1: Market Selection

The highest-leverage decision. Evaluate every market against four criteria:

**Pain** — Is the market in active, urgent pain? Not mild inconvenience — real pain that costs them money, time, reputation, or sleep. In B2B, this means pain tied to revenue loss, operational chaos, competitive disadvantage, or leadership pressure. If you have to convince someone they have a problem, the market is wrong.

**Purchasing Power** — Can they pay, and can they pay premium prices? A market in tremendous pain but without budget is a charity project. For B2B, this is why revenue thresholds matter — targeting businesses at a certain ARR ensures the economics support premium pricing.

**Easy to Target** — Can you find and reach them efficiently? Do they congregate in identifiable places — LinkedIn groups, industry conferences, specific publications, online communities, professional associations? If the audience is scattered and invisible, acquisition economics break down regardless of how good the offer is.

**Growing** — Is the market expanding? A growing market provides tailwind. Even mediocre execution can work when demand is expanding faster than supply. A shrinking market means fighting for a smaller pie no matter how good your offer is.

**Diagnostic questions for campaign analysis:**
- Does the campaign target a market that passes all four criteria?
- Is the pain being addressed active and urgent, or theoretical?
- Is the target audience at a revenue/size threshold that supports the price point?
- Can this audience be efficiently reached through the channels being used?
- Is this market growing, stable, or contracting?

---

### Step 2: Define the Dream Outcome

The transformation you are selling — not the mechanism, not the deliverable, not the feature set. The end state the buyer wants to live in.

**Rules for a well-defined dream outcome:**

1. It must be describable as a specific moment. Not "grow revenue" but "looking at your dashboard on a Friday afternoon and realizing you've already hit your monthly number with a week left." If you cannot picture a scene, you are still at the vague benefit level.

2. It must connect to status. Who in the buyer's world will notice the transformation? Their board, their spouse, their competitors, their team, their investors? The dream outcome should make someone else in their life react. Use the "what, who, when" framework — what is the status shift, who is awarding the status, and when does it happen?

3. It must pass the "pay to skip the line" test. If the dream outcome is vivid and desirable enough, the perceived value goes up, which supports premium pricing. A weak dream outcome forces price competition.

4. It must be expressed in the buyer's language, not the seller's jargon. The best dream outcomes sound like how the buyer would describe success to a friend over drinks.

**Diagnostic questions for campaign analysis:**
- Is the dream outcome specific and vivid, or vague and generic?
- Can you identify the specific moment the buyer would experience that tells them "it worked"?
- Does the dream outcome connect to status within the buyer's world?
- Is it described in the buyer's language or the seller's jargon?

---

### Step 3: List All Obstacles

Every reason the buyer might not achieve the dream outcome. No filtering, no solving — just problems. The goal is exhaustive volume.

**Layer the obstacles chronologically:**

**Before they start:**
- Confusion about first steps
- Overwhelm from too many options
- Fear of choosing the wrong solution
- Past failures creating skepticism
- Internal politics and inability to get buy-in (critical in B2B)
- Organizational scar tissue from previous vendor failures

**During the process:**
- Loss of motivation mid-implementation
- Technical problems and unexpected complexity
- Distraction from urgent competing priorities
- Team adoption and change management friction
- The "this is harder than we thought" moment
- Data migration, integration, and system conflicts

**After "completion":**
- Maintenance and sustainability concerns
- Backsliding to old behaviors
- Staff turnover undoing institutional knowledge
- Scaling challenges as they grow past the solution

**Hidden obstacles (the ones they won't say on a sales call):**
- Imposter syndrome
- Embarrassment about how bad things currently are
- Spouse or partner skepticism about the investment
- Fear that even if it works, they cannot sustain it
- Ego — not wanting to admit they need help

**Diagnostic questions for campaign analysis:**
- Does the offer address obstacles at all three stages (before, during, after)?
- Are hidden/emotional obstacles being acknowledged?
- Are there obvious gaps — obstacles the buyer will face that the offer doesn't address?
- Is the ad copy calling out obstacles the buyer recognizes, or generic marketing language?

---

### Step 4: Turn Problems into Solutions

Every obstacle from Step 3 gets flipped into a solution. The question for each: "What would I need to provide so this problem disappears for the buyer?"

**Key principles:**

- Solve ALL the problems, not just the core one. Most businesses solve the main thing and leave the buyer to figure out everything surrounding it. Grand Slam Offers solve the surrounding problems too — that is what makes them feel like a category of one.

- Solutions come in different forms. Some are products, some are services, some are just information. A one-page quickstart guide can be as valuable as a full service engagement if it removes the right obstacle. Not everything has to be a deliverable.

- Some solutions remove obstacles entirely. Some just reduce them. Reducing friction is still valuable. A dedicated onboarding person who holds the buyer's hand through implementation still takes time, but it feels effortless — that shrinks effort and sacrifice in the value equation.

- Name each solution like a standalone product using the MAGIC formula (see below). Named, packaged solutions feel like more value than unnamed features. Each named component becomes a potential bonus in the enhancer stack.

- Watch for solutions that address hidden obstacles. Private onboarding instead of group (addresses embarrassment). Strong guarantee structure (addresses fear of failure). These quiet solutions often do more for conversion than flashy features because they neutralize objections the buyer was never going to voice.

**Diagnostic questions for campaign analysis:**
- Does the offer solve only the core problem, or surrounding problems too?
- Are there low-cost, high-perceived-value solutions (templates, guides, checklists) that could be added?
- Are hidden obstacles being addressed through offer structure (guarantees, private delivery, etc.)?
- Is each solution component named and positioned as its own valuable thing?

---

### Step 5: The Delivery Cube

Each solution component gets a delivery structure across three dimensions:

**Dimension 1 — Level of Involvement:**

| Level | Description | Sell Difficulty | Fulfillment Cost | Value Equation Impact |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------------|
| DIY (Do It Yourself) | Buyer gets tools and executes | Hardest to sell | Cheapest to fulfill | Higher effort & sacrifice for buyer |
| DWY (Done With You) | Collaborative — coaching, consulting, partnership | Middle ground | Moderate cost | Balanced — leverage without full burden |
| DFY (Done For You) | Seller handles everything | Easiest to sell | Most expensive | Lowest effort & sacrifice for buyer |

Different solutions within the same offer can live at different levels. Core deliverable might be DFY while onboarding support is DWY and maintenance training is DIY.

**Dimension 2 — Medium of Delivery:**
- One-on-one vs. small group vs. one-to-many
- In person vs. virtual vs. hybrid
- Live vs. recorded vs. asynchronous
- Written vs. video vs. audio vs. software

The art is finding mediums that feel high-touch without requiring seller time for every customer. Small group formats, cohort models, and async systems with strategic live touchpoints hit this balance.

**Dimension 3 — Speed:**
- How fast does the buyer get the result?
- Can the delivery be restructured so they get a quick win early, even if the full result takes longer?
- Speed directly attacks time delay in the value equation.
- Speed is an underrated differentiator — the faster offer wins even if the slower offer eventually delivers more total value.

**Diagnostic questions for campaign analysis:**
- Is the delivery model appropriate for the price point and market?
- Are there opportunities to shift components along the DIY-DFY spectrum to increase value or reduce cost?
- Does the buyer get a quick win early in the process?
- Are high-touch elements being used strategically or wastefully?

---

### Step 6: Trim, Stack, and Enhance

**The Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum:**

On one end: things that are easy to sell but hard to fulfill. On the other: easy to fulfill but hard to sell. The sweet spot is high perceived value to the buyer but low cost and complexity to deliver.

**Trimming rules:**
- If you cannot deliver it consistently at quality, cut it. A mediocre solution is worse than no solution — it breaks said-do correspondence and erodes trust.
- Look for solutions deliverable once but consumed many times (templates, toolkits, recorded trainings, frameworks) — high perceived value, near-zero marginal cost.
- Apply the "would I pay for this standalone?" test. If a component could not stand alone, it is not adding enough perceived value to justify delivery cost.
- Watch for scope creep disguised as generosity. A tight offer with four components delivered exceptionally outperforms a bloated offer with ten components delivered inconsistently. Proof compounds from the tight version and erodes from the bloated one.

**The enhancer stack comes next — detailed in the following section.**

---

## The Enhancer Stack

Four categories of psychological triggers layered on top of structural value.

### Scarcity (Quantity-Based Limits)

Caps on how many people can buy. Only 20 spots, only 50 licenses, only 10 clients this quarter.

**Rules:**
- Must be real. If you say 20 spots and sell 40, you destroy credibility for every future launch.
- When someone wants to buy after the cap, you say no. That person becomes your most eager buyer next time.
- You can limit the total offer, a specific bonus, or a tier within the offer. Capping just the DFY tier while leaving DWY open gives scarcity on the premium end without leaving money on the table.
- Undersell demand so you always have more later. Overselling and having nobody believe your limits is where you lose power.

### Urgency (Time-Based Limits)

When the offer expires, not how many are available.

**Rules:**
- Must be real. If the price goes up Monday and it doesn't, you've trained the market to ignore your deadlines.
- The promotion ends Friday. The bonus disappears at midnight. The price increases on the first.
- Can run independently of scarcity or together. Both simultaneously is strongest but hardest to maintain authentically.

### Bonuses (Breaking the Offer into Parts and Stacking)

Each solution component gets named and presented as its own valuable thing with its own value anchor.

**Rules:**
- Use the MAGIC naming formula for each bonus.
- Each bonus should address a specific obstacle from the obstacle list.
- The buyer should feel like they are getting five or six things instead of one.
- Sometimes the bonus is actually worth more than the core offer — counterintuitive but powerful. If the bonus alone would solve a major problem, saying no to the whole package feels irrational.

### Guarantees (Removing Risk)

Four types, stackable:

**Unconditional** — No questions asked, full refund, no conditions. Lowest barrier to entry. Communicates supreme confidence. Works best when fulfillment is strong enough that refund rates are negligible.

**Conditional** — "Do X, and if you don't get Y within Z time, we refund you." Implies a specific outcome and timeframe. Filters out people who will not do the work, protecting fulfillment and proof.

**Anti-Guarantee** — All sales final. Works when fulfillment cost is high or commitment itself is part of the value. "We don't offer refunds because we invest heavily in your success from day one and only want people who are fully committed." Increases perceived value for the right buyer.

**Implied** — Performance-based structures (rev-share, pay-on-results). Strongest signal of confidence because you are betting on yourself. Not explicitly called a guarantee but the structure removes risk.

**Stacking guarantees:** Unconditional for the first 30 days, then conditional for the remaining 60. Or conditional on the core outcome plus implied on the performance component. Stacking feels over the top, which is why it works — nobody else does it.

**Diagnostic questions for campaign analysis:**
- Which enhancers are present? Which are missing?
- Are scarcity and urgency claims legitimate and enforceable?
- Are bonuses named, valued separately, and tied to specific obstacles?
- What guarantee structure is in place? Could it be strengthened or stacked?
- Does the enhancer mix match the market? (Enterprise B2B may need less urgency/scarcity and more proof/guarantees; SMB may respond more to urgency.)

## The MAGIC Naming Formula

For naming offers, bonuses, and components so the name itself does selling work.

| Letter | Meaning | Function |
|--------|---------|----------|
| M | Magnetic Reason Why | The name hints at why this exists or why it works — a built-in "because" |
| A | Announce the Avatar | Who is this for? The right person self-selects from the name |
| G | Give the Goal | What dream outcome does this deliver? The destination, not the mechanism |
| I | Indicate the Time | How fast? A time element creates urgency and reduces perceived time delay |
| C | Container Word | What is this thing? Blueprint, system, accelerator, sprint, protocol, toolkit, playbook |

**Example transformation:**
- Before: "Our Onboarding Program"
- After: "The SaaS Founder's 14-Day Revenue Launch System"
  - Avatar: SaaS Founder
  - Time: 14-Day
  - Goal: Revenue Launch
  - Container: System
  - Magnetic reason why (implied): It is a launch system because the first two weeks matter most

Not all five elements are required every time. Forcing all five can make the name clunky. Use MAGIC as a checklist to run names against, not a rigid template.

**Apply at every level:** The offer name, every bonus name, every component name. Each is a mini headline. Since headlines are 80 cents of the ad dollar, naming is doing persuasion work before anyone reads a single line of copy.

---

## Core Operating Principles

These principles from Hormozi should inform every analysis and recommendation:

- **Niche down ruthlessly.** Broad targeting creates vanilla messaging. Tight niches allow specificity, and specificity creates compelling copy.
- **Proof beats promise.** Always. Competitors can copy your offer but cannot copy your proof. Proof is earned and compounds over time.
- **Charge premium prices.** Value-driven pricing, not price-driven competition. The value equation supports premium pricing when the numerator is high enough.
- **Sell the transformation, not the mechanism.** The buyer does not care about the process — they care about the end state.
- **Over-deliver early.** Quick wins create proof, momentum, and belief. They increase perceived likelihood of achievement.
- **Create flow. Monetize flow. Then add friction.** Start by over-delivering to generate demand and proof. Optimize for profitability later. The Grand Slam Offer is not launched fully formed on day one — it compounds as you deliver results and learn.

# PART TWO: THE 12 AD COPY HACKS

These are operational tools for writing and evaluating ad copy. They are a toolkit, not a rigid checklist — different ads pull from different combinations depending on the objective.

## Functional Layers

The 12 hacks serve four distinct jobs within any piece of copy. Understanding which layer each hack serves allows the agent to diagnose which layer is weakest in any underperforming ad.

### The Attention Layer — Getting Them to Stop

**Hack 1: Headlines Come First**

David Ogilvy: once you have written your headline, you have spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar. The headline is the single highest-leverage element of any ad.

**Principles:**
- Curiosity is king. Different is ideal. Sexy works.
- A headline that moves click-through rate from 1% to 3% triples the audience for everything downstream. No other element in the funnel can typically achieve a 2-3x lift.
- All ads have headlines — there are just bad ones and good ones. Not writing a headline means you have a bad one by default.
- Headlines are different wrapping paper for the same offer. You can run the same offer indefinitely with fresh headlines.
- The headline should grab the reader by the throat.

**Testing headlines:**
- Look at organic content performance (yours and others') — if a hook gets engagement organically, it will likely work paid.
- Steal from different industries. A headline pattern beaten to death in weight loss might feel fresh in B2B SaaS. Same structure, new context.
- Look at the first 3 seconds of top-performing video ads — what are they saying and what is happening visually?

**Headline formula:** "How to [good thing] without [bad thing] even if you [biggest fear/insecurity]."

Example: "How to speak confidently in front of strangers without ever practicing even if you're deathly afraid of speaking in public."

**Hack 3: Always Call Out Who You're Looking For**

Saying who you are NOT looking for is even more powerful than saying who you are looking for. Disqualification implies authority and creates polarization.

**Principles:**
- When you say no to customers, you polarize the right customers toward you. Vanilla attracts no one.
- Never advertise to the masses. Advertise to one person. That person should think "How does he know my secrets?"
- Specificity over marketing lingo. Not "business owners" — describe the moment. "If you've got three reps sitting idle because your pipeline dried up last month."
- Replace vague words with experiential descriptions. Not "Are you overweight?" but "Do you have trouble getting upstairs without sweating in places you didn't know you could?"

**Levels of awareness (tailor the call-out accordingly):**

| Level | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Completely Unaware | Must hook with curiosity | Pure curiosity-driven headline |
| Problem Aware | Knows they have a problem | "Do you get up at night to pee more than two times?" |
| Solution Aware | Has tried solutions | "Tried an ad agency and it hasn't worked?" |
| Product Aware | Knows specific product types | "Tried creatine monohydrate vs. HCL?" |
| Most Aware | Knows you and your product | Make an offer directly |

**The moment principle:** Think about the specific moments someone experiences the problem. Not "loneliness" but the moment they eat dinner alone for the third night in a row. Not "low sales" but the moment they look at their pipeline on Monday and there is nothing in it. Pain is remembered in moments. Copy that pinpoints fresh, recent moments lowers the action threshold because the pain is top of mind.

---

### The Trust Layer — Getting Them to Believe You

**Hack 2: Say What Only You Can Say**

Offensive proof. Specific numbers, specific results, specific experience that nobody else can claim.

**Principles:**
- Proof always outdoes promise.
- Competitors can copy your offer but they cannot copy your proof. Proof must be earned.
- You control the pond you are one-of-a-kind in. If you are the only person who has helped vegan powerlifting moms over 35, say so.
- In an AI-saturated world, story and verified real-world experience become even more important. Nobody can take your story away.
- The question at the top of everyone's mind: "Why should I listen to you?" Answer it with what you have actually done.

**For campaign analysis:** Does the ad contain claims that only this business could make? Or could any competitor swap in their name and the ad would still work? If the latter, the ad lacks proof differentiation.

**Hack 5: Damaging Admissions**

Defensive proof. Disarming skepticism by saying what the prospect is already thinking.

**Principles:**
- Research shows that admitting bias upfront makes people MORE likely to believe you, not less.
- Own your flaws. Your product is not perfect — say so. What you sacrifice in promise you gain in trust. Trust trumps promise.
- Think about referrals: you barely have to promise anything because trust is already established. Damaging admissions build that same trust with strangers.

**The linguistic structure:** "[Damaging truth] but [the thing you want them to believe]."

The statement after "but" is what gets emphasized and believed. The damage before "but" buys credibility for the claim.

**Examples:**
- "Our websites are ugly as hell but they convert."
- "Our systems are incredibly boring but they work."
- "Viagra only works for 4 to 6 hours but you're going to have the time of your life."
- "Our gym has terrible parking, the AC is unreliable, and our equipment isn't the best. But our sessions are more fun than anyone else's because it's all we've got."

**Flipped (wrong) structure:** "These markers write four times longer than others but they smell like crap." This emphasizes the crap smell. The order matters.

**For campaign analysis:** Identify the ad's biggest claims. Are there any damaging admissions that make those claims more believable? If the ad is all promise with no admission of limitation, it is likely triggering skepticism. Write down all the flaws, all the complaints, the things the business knows it could do better — then use them as trust-builders.

**Hack 9: Implied Authority**

Ambient proof. Social proof, longevity, volume, and credentials that create a background hum of legitimacy.

**Principles:**
- Longevity signals legitimacy. "We've been in business for 40 years" is powerful because it is a rare feat.
- Volume of proof beats perfection. 3,000 reviews at 4.6 stars beats 3 reviews at 5 stars. Volume implies experience.
- Aggregate credentials count. Four co-founders with five years each can say "over 20 years of combined advertising experience."
- Small awards and lists matter. "Best boot camp in Vegas" creates a conversational opener and implied authority even if few people know the list exists.
- Document and substantiate everything. Every client helped, every result achieved — it all compounds into authority.

**For campaign analysis:** What implied authority elements are present in the ad? Testimonial count, years in business, notable clients, awards, media mentions, volume of customers served? What is missing that could be added?

---

### The Desire Layer — Making Them Want It

**Hack 6: Show Don't Tell**

Do not describe benefits abstractly. Describe the specific moment the buyer will experience the benefit.

**Principles:**
- Instead of "get more sales" — describe the experience of getting more sales: running out of order forms because customers are reading their credit cards too fast.
- Instead of "get more leads" — describe the overwhelm of having too many phones ring and not enough salespeople.
- Describe the future problems they would prefer to have over their current problems. If you can describe problems they have not thought of yet, it implies you have experienced them or helped others experience them, which increases believability.
- The moments principle applies here too. What is the moment they realize the transformation has occurred? What does it look like? What does it feel like? Describe that scene.

**Why this produces unique copy:** Different people describe the same moment differently. Your description will always sound different from competitors because you are painting a picture, not using the same generic benefit language everyone else uses.

**For campaign analysis:** Is the ad describing vague outcomes ("grow revenue," "save time," "get more leads") or specific experiential moments? If vague, rewrite using moment-based descriptions.

**Hack 7: Tie Benefits to Status**

Take benefits one level deeper by connecting them to who in the buyer's world will give them status.

**Principles:**
- Not just "cook meals fast and easy" but "so fast and easy that all your friends will wonder how you found the time to be fit and cook for your family."
- The "what, who, when" framework: What is the status shift? Who is awarding the status? When does it happen?
- Status-givers to consider: family, friends, colleagues, co-workers, rivals, competitors, industry peers, board members, investors, spouse.
- Think across time: past, present, future. How will this create status for them with their spouse today? In the future? Reflecting on the past?
- This gives you endless copy variations and angles from a single benefit.

**Sentence structures:** "So that you can..." / "So that your [status-giver] will..." / "So that your friend's mouths will drop with envy."

**For campaign analysis:** Does the ad connect benefits to a specific person or group who will notice? If benefits are described in isolation (just about the buyer themselves), there is a status layer missing that would increase emotional resonance.

---

### The Action Layer — Getting Them to Move

**Hack 4: Always Have a Reason Why**

The word "because" is one of the most influential in the English language.

**Principles:**
- Research from Cialdini: having any reason — even a nonsensical one — significantly increases compliance compared to no reason.
- The fraternity party planner standard: the bar for having a reason is incredibly low. Timmy got his wisdom teeth out — party. It's Tuesday — party. Your dog's birthday — promotion.
- Bonus points if the reason ties to urgency or scarcity.
- Every line of copy sells reading the next line. Those lines sell the click. The click sells the landing page. The headline sells the opt-in. Sell one step at a time. A confused mind does not buy. A confused mind does not click.

**For campaign analysis:** Does the ad or offer have a stated reason for existing right now? If there is a promotion, is there a reason given (even a simple one)? Is the ad trying to sell multiple steps at once, or one clear next step?

**Hack 8: Use Urgency and Scarcity**

Urgency is a function of time. Scarcity is a function of quantity. They are different levers.

| Type | Mechanism | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Urgency | Time-based limit | Promotion ends at midnight Friday |
| Scarcity | Quantity-based limit | Only 100 spots available |
| Both | Combined | Only 50 spots, and enrollment closes Friday |

**The one rule:** It must be legitimate. If it is not real, you erode trust for every future campaign. When someone wants to buy after the limit, say no. That person becomes your most eager buyer next time. Undersell demand to maintain power.

**Hack 10: Always Have a PS Statement**

PS = Power Sentence. The second most-read part of any ad or email after the headline.

**Three strong PS strategies:**
1. A reward for reading — jokes, light content, something fun that trains people to read to the bottom. Then when you do use the PS as a CTA, they click because you have trained the behavior.
2. A disqualification — "By the way, if you're just getting started, this isn't for you. This is only for people doing X. Click here." Filters and polarizes.
3. A recap — All main persuasion points in one sentence. For people who scrolled to the bottom (which many do).

PPS is the third most-read element. Use it if needed.

**Hack 11: Clear CTAs (Calls to Action)**

Tell people exactly what to do next and exactly what will happen when they do it.

**Principles:**
- Not "you know what to do" — they do not. "Click the button. On the next page fill in your information. Then select a convenient time."
- When you tell someone to take a step, and what you said would happen actually happens, you increase "said-do correspondence." You gain influence. They are more likely to follow your next instruction.
- Martha Stewart effect: she gave recipes, people followed them, the cake turned out good, family praised the cake (status!), and she gained influence for the next instruction.
- Embed this within the persuasion process: make promise, keep promise, make promise, keep promise. They must take action and your prediction must come true.
- Outline steps one, two, three. Outline what happens next. When it does, they believe you the next time.

**For campaign analysis:** Is the CTA specific and concrete, or vague? Does the ad tell the buyer exactly what happens after they click? Is there a multi-step CTA that builds said-do correspondence?

**Hack 12: Third Grade Reading Level**

Most copy does not convert because people have to pause to understand it. The moment they pause, they stop.

**Assume the prospect:**
- Is in a rush
- Does not care about what you have
- Reads below a third grade level
- Is distracted

**Rules:**
- Short sentences. Small words. Big promises. Big proof.
- Few adverbs, better verbs. Not "he quickly ran" — "he sprinted."
- Word concision: as few words as possible to communicate the point.
- Simplicity trumps concision. If a simpler word requires two words instead of one fancy word, use the simpler option.
- Fancy equals friction. Simple equals sales.
- Put every ad through a free reading level tool. Edit until it reads at third grade level.
- Great copy makes the words fade into the background. Nobody thinks about the words — they just understand and are persuaded.
- Writing at ninth grade level automatically disqualifies roughly two-thirds of readers.

**For campaign analysis:** Run the copy through a readability test. Flag any sentence that requires re-reading, any jargon that is not the buyer's own daily language, any sentence over 15 words. Simplify ruthlessly.

---

### The Multiplier — Humor

Not one of the 12 but a bonus amplifier. Entertainment to go broad, education to go deep.

**Principles:**
- The highest-converting ad of all time (Old Spice) was funny. It took a non-market-leader and made it the market share dominator.
- If you can entertain and educate at the same time, you win. Entertainment broadens reach. Education changes behavior.
- Humor is the first thing to cut if it does not work. You can make ads that convert with the other 12 alone. Humor is extra sauce.
- Test humor the way comedians build specials: test widely, find the 8 minutes out of 60 that work, frontload those, and iterate until the entire set converts.
- If humor is present but the ad is not converting, remove the humor first — it may be distracting from the persuasion.

# PART THREE: B2B STATIC AD ARCHETYPES

Three ad archetypes mapped to Hormozi's copy hacks. Each archetype pulls from a different cluster of the 12 hacks. When analyzing a B2B ad, identify which archetype it is attempting to be, then evaluate it against the relevant hack cluster.

## Archetype 1: Bottleneck Ads

**Primary purpose:** Surface normalized pain and make the buyer feel seen.

**Core hacks used:** Call out who you're looking for (#3), show don't tell (#6), damaging admissions (#5), headlines first (#1).

**Characteristics:**
- Temporal specificity of pain — pinpoint the exact moment the buyer experiences the problem. Not last quarter, not generally. This week. Monday morning. The moment they opened the dashboard. The moment they looked behind the bar.
- Surface normalized problems — things the buyer has accepted as "just how it is" that are actually solvable. The goal is to make them suddenly aware of pain they have been tolerating.
- Hard call-outs — specific enough that the right buyer feels like you are reading their mind, and the wrong buyer feels no connection. "If you've got three reps sitting idle because your pipeline dried up last month" — not "struggling with sales."
- Damaging admissions woven in — acknowledge an uncomfortable truth about the industry or the buyer's situation that builds trust for the solution presented.
- Relief-focused CTAs — the action step should feel like relief from the pain just surfaced, not an additional burden.

**Diagnostic checklist:**
- Does the ad describe a specific, recent moment of pain?
- Is the pain described in experiential language, not marketing jargon?
- Would a buyer who does NOT have this problem ignore the ad? (If it speaks to everyone, it speaks to no one.)
- Is there a damaging admission that builds trust?
- Does the CTA feel like relief?

---

## Archetype 2: Dream Outcome Ads

**Primary purpose:** Paint the after-state so vividly the buyer feels the transformation before buying.

**Core hacks used:** Show don't tell (#6), tie benefits to status (#7), headlines first (#1), say what only you can say (#2).

**Characteristics:**
- Vivid after-state moments — not "you'll have more revenue" but "your CFO asking YOU how you did it." The moment principle applied to the positive end state.
- Status-coded outcomes — every benefit connected to someone specific who will notice. Board members, spouse, competitors, team. Using the "what, who, when" framework across multiple status-givers and time periods.
- Proof anchors — specific numbers, case studies, or results woven into the aspiration to ground it in reality. The dream outcome becomes more believable when proof is embedded.
- Aspirational problem framing — describe the "problems you'd rather have." Having too many leads, needing to hire faster, outgrowing your current office. These future problems imply success.

**Diagnostic checklist:**
- Does the ad describe a specific after-state moment, or just vague benefits?
- Is status explicitly connected to someone in the buyer's world?
- Is there proof embedded that makes the aspiration believable?
- Are "problems you'd rather have" used to imply the transformation?

---

## Archetype 3: Proof Ads

**Primary purpose:** Overwhelm skepticism with evidence that demands belief.

**Core hacks used:** Say what only you can say (#2), implied authority (#9), call out who you're looking for (#3), headlines first (#1).

**Characteristics:**
- Falsifiable specifics — claims that are specific enough to be disproven, which makes them more believable. Not "we've helped many businesses" but "we've helped 43 SaaS companies between $1M and $5M ARR hit their next funding round in the last 7 years."
- Layered implied authority — longevity, volume, notable clients, awards, media mentions, credential stacking. Multiple proof signals compounding.
- "Say what only you can say" as the foundation — every claim should be something a competitor could not simply copy and paste into their own ad.
- Qualification-language CTAs — the CTA itself implies the buyer must meet a standard. "If you're serious about X, click here." The qualification makes taking action feel like joining an exclusive group, not submitting to a sales pitch.

**Diagnostic checklist:**
- Are the claims specific enough to be falsifiable?
- Could a competitor swap their name into this ad and it would still work? (If yes, the proof is too generic.)
- How many layers of implied authority are present?
- Does the CTA use qualification language?

# PART FOUR: THE CAMPAIGN ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

When the agent receives an offer, ad, or campaign to analyze, follow this diagnostic sequence.

## Step 1: Market Diagnosis

Before touching copy or creative, evaluate the market.
- Does the target market pass all four criteria (pain, purchasing power, easy to target, growing)?
- Is the niche tight enough to allow specific, experiential language?
- Is the market selection the real problem, or is it solid?

If the market is wrong, no amount of copy optimization will fix the campaign. Flag this first.

## Step 2: Offer Diagnosis

Evaluate the offer against the value equation.
- Dream Outcome: Is it vivid, specific, moment-based, and status-connected?
- Perceived Likelihood: Is there proof, guarantee structure, and implied authority supporting believability?
- Time Delay: How fast does the buyer see results? Are quick wins built in?
- Effort & Sacrifice: How much does the buyer have to do? Are obstacles being removed for them?

Identify which of the four levers is weakest. That is where the biggest improvement lives.

## Step 3: Offer Structure Diagnosis

Evaluate the components.
- Are obstacles at all three stages (before, during, after) being addressed?
- Are solutions appropriately structured via the Delivery Cube?
- Is the enhancer stack present and appropriate for the market?
- Are components named using the MAGIC formula?
- Is there scope creep, or is the offer tight and deliverable?

## Step 4: Copy Diagnosis

Identify the ad archetype (Bottleneck, Dream Outcome, or Proof) and evaluate against the relevant hack cluster.

Run the full 12-hack diagnostic:

| # | Hack | Question |
|---|------|----------|
| 1 | Headlines First | Is the headline strong enough to stop the scroll? Could it 2-3x click-through? |
| 2 | Say What Only You Can Say | Does the ad contain claims only this business could make? |
| 3 | Call Out Who You're Looking For | Is the audience clearly identified — and are the wrong people clearly excluded? |
| 4 | Reason Why | Is there a stated reason for this offer existing right now? |
| 5 | Damaging Admissions | Are flaws owned to build trust for the core claim? |
| 6 | Show Don't Tell | Are outcomes described as specific moments, not vague benefits? |
| 7 | Tie Benefits to Status | Is status connected to a specific person/group who will notice? |
| 8 | Urgency & Scarcity | Are time or quantity limits present and legitimate? |
| 9 | Implied Authority | What proof signals are present — longevity, volume, awards, clients? |
| 10 | PS Statement | Is the second-most-read element being used? Is it strong? |
| 11 | Clear CTA | Is the next step specific? Does the buyer know exactly what happens next? |
| 12 | Third Grade Reading Level | Is the copy simple enough that someone rushed and distracted would understand instantly? |
| Bonus | Humor | Is humor present? Is it helping or distracting? |

## Step 5: Optimization Recommendations

Prioritize recommendations by the hierarchy of leverage:
1. Market fixes first (if applicable)
2. Offer structure fixes second
3. Copy and creative fixes third

Within copy fixes, prioritize by the functional layer:
1. Attention layer (headlines) — highest leverage
2. Trust layer (proof, damaging admissions, authority) — biggest conversion impact
3. Desire layer (show don't tell, status) — deepens engagement
4. Action layer (CTA, urgency, reason why) — closes the loop

Always provide specific rewrites, not just abstract suggestions. Show the before and after. Demonstrate the principle in action.

# PART FIVE: QUICK REFERENCE DRILL

For rapid application, here is a single-offer drill format. Given any B2B offer, cycle through each hack and generate one angle for each.

**Example: Fractional CFO service targeting SaaS founders at $1-5M ARR.**

| Hack | Angle |
|------|-------|
| 1. Headline | "Your bookkeeper is not a CFO." |
| 2. Say What Only You Can Say | "We've helped 43 SaaS companies between $1M and $5M ARR hit their next funding round." |
| 3. Call Out | "If you're pre-revenue, this isn't for you. If you're past $1M and still making financial decisions off a spreadsheet your co-founder built in 2021 — keep reading." |
| 4. Reason Why | "We're opening five spots this quarter because we just brought on a second partner." |
| 5. Damaging Admission | "We're not cheap. We don't do your bookkeeping. We won't fix your invoicing. But we will make sure you don't run out of cash before your next raise." |
| 6. Show Don't Tell | "Opening your laptop Monday morning and knowing exactly how many months of runway you have without asking anyone." |
| 7. Status | "Walk into your next board meeting and actually want them to ask about your numbers." |
| 8. Urgency/Scarcity | "Five engagements per quarter. Three are already spoken for." |
| 9. Implied Authority | "Seven years. 43 SaaS companies. Zero surprised by their burn rate." |
| 10. PS | "PS — If your last board meeting included the phrase 'we'll have to get back to you on that,' you already know you need this." |
| 11. CTA | "Click below. Fill out three questions. We'll send a 10-minute audit of your financial blind spots before we ever get on a call." |
| 12. Reading Level | Every line above: short sentences, small words, no jargon beyond what the avatar uses daily. |
| Bonus: Humor | "Your investors don't think your vibes-based forecasting model is as charming as you do." |

This drill format can be applied to any offer to rapidly generate copy angles across all 12 hacks.

# APPENDIX: KEY TERMS AND DEFINITIONS

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Grand Slam Offer | An offer so good people feel stupid saying no — built by solving all obstacles, structuring delivery smartly, and layering enhancers |
| Value Equation | (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice) |
| Delivery Cube | Three-dimensional framework for structuring solution delivery: involvement level (DIY/DWY/DFY) × medium × speed |
| Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum | The spectrum from easy-to-sell/hard-to-fulfill to hard-to-sell/easy-to-fulfill; the sweet spot is high perceived value with low delivery cost |
| MAGIC Formula | Naming framework: Magnetic reason why, Announce avatar, Give goal, Indicate time, Container word |
| Said-Do Correspondence | When you tell someone what will happen, they take action, and what you said happens — building influence for the next instruction |
| Damaging Admission | Owning a flaw or limitation to build trust for the claim that follows |
| Implied Authority | Indirect proof signals (longevity, volume, awards, social proof) that create a background hum of legitimacy |
| Starving Crowd | A market with urgent, active pain and purchasing power — the most important factor in business success |
| Bottleneck Ad | B2B ad archetype that surfaces normalized pain through temporal specificity and experiential language |
| Dream Outcome Ad | B2B ad archetype that paints the after-state vividly, connecting benefits to status |
| Proof Ad | B2B ad archetype that overwhelms skepticism with falsifiable specifics and layered authority |
| The Moment Principle | The practice of describing pain or outcomes as specific experiential moments rather than vague abstractions |
| Temporal Specificity | Pinpointing when the pain or outcome occurs — this week, Monday morning, the moment they opened the dashboard |
| Status-Coding | Connecting benefits to the specific person or group who will notice and award status to the buyer |
